Design Leadership Global Systems Thinking Strategy

The Coherence Project

A global design consistency initiative — unifying design language across North America and international markets while preserving regional flexibility.

Role

Design Systems Lead

Timeline

9 months, 2025

Tools

Figma, Confluence, FigJam

Impact

65% adoption increase

01 — Context & Challenge

Global products. Regional realities. No shared foundation.

Digital products spanning multiple geographies face a structural tension: global consistency enables brand recognition, reduces build cost, and accelerates delivery — but regional markets have legitimate differences in user expectations, regulatory requirements, and cultural context that a rigid global standard can't absorb.

Without a principled framework for navigating that tension, teams default to one of two failure modes: rigid uniformity that breaks in market, or unconstrained divergence that defeats the purpose of a shared system entirely. The Coherence Project was about finding the principled middle.

Coherence isn't uniformity. The goal was a design language flexible enough to be locally right and consistent enough to be globally recognizable.

02 — Research & Discovery

Auditing consistency at global scale.

I began with a consistency audit across 14 product surfaces spanning North America, Europe, and Asia-Pacific markets. The audit scored each surface against a baseline set of design principles — typography, color, spacing, interaction patterns — to identify where divergence was intentional and value-adding versus accidental and costly.

  • Only 34% of design decisions that differed by region were intentionally differentiated
  • 66% of regional variation was incidental — teams solving the same problem independently
  • 3 distinct "dialects" of the design system had emerged across regions with no cross-pollination
  • Regional teams reported feeling disconnected from the central design system's evolution

03 — Design Process

Building a framework for intentional flexibility.

The core design decision was establishing a three-tier model: global (non-negotiable, brand-critical), regional (intentionally adaptable, within defined parameters), and local (free variation, documented for potential global promotion).

This gave every team a clear answer to the question "can we change this?" — and a path to contribute regional innovations back to the global system rather than having them die in isolation. I facilitated alignment workshops with regional leads in each market to co-author the tier classifications, ensuring buy-in rather than compliance.

04 — Outcomes

Consistency that scales, flexibility that respects context.

  • Design system adoption up 65% globally, with highest growth in previously disengaged regional markets
  • Cross-team delivery speed increased 40% for new product surfaces using the tiered framework
  • 12 regional design patterns elevated to the global system — first time this had happened in the program's history
  • Governance model adopted as the standard for future global/regional design coordination

The most enduring outcome was a shift in how teams across regions related to each other — from parallel silos to collaborating contributors. The framework gave everyone a shared vocabulary for a conversation that had previously been stuck.
