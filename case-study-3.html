Service Design Workshop Facilitation Cross-functional Strategy

Mapping the Invisible

Service blueprinting to surface the friction nobody could see — aligning 6 teams, 3 workshops, and one shared understanding.

Role

Service Designer / Facilitator

Timeline

3 months, 2024

Tools

Miro, FigJam, Figma

Impact

6 teams aligned

01 — Context & Challenge

Everyone owned a piece. Nobody owned the whole.

A complex insurance product touched six distinct teams — underwriting, product, engineering, operations, compliance, and customer support. Each team had a reasonable view of their own slice of the process. None had a view of the whole.

The symptoms were everywhere: delays at handoff points, duplicated work, customers caught in communication gaps between teams, and escalation loops that nobody could trace to a root cause. The problem wasn't capability — it was visibility. Nobody could see what they couldn't see.

The goal wasn't to redesign anything yet. It was to build a shared map of reality that every team could read, challenge, and own together.

02 — Research & Discovery

Interviewing the seams, not just the centers.

I ran 22 stakeholder interviews focused specifically on handoff moments — what each team expected to receive, what they actually received, and what they passed forward without knowing if it would land correctly.

The pre-workshop audit revealed:

  • 11 distinct handoff points in the product lifecycle, only 4 of which had documented expectations
  • 3 teams were solving the same data gap independently, without awareness of each other
  • Customer support fielded questions rooted in process failures that happened 3–4 steps earlier
  • No team had a complete picture of the customer experience timeline

03 — The Workshops

Designing the room before designing the map.

I designed three workshops, each with a distinct purpose: surface (what does each team believe is happening?), reconcile (where do those beliefs conflict?), and resolve (what decisions and owners do we need to close the gaps?).

Each session used structured facilitation — not open discussion, but constrained exercises that forced specificity. Swim-lane mapping, assumption surfacing, and red/green tagging of handoff confidence levels. The structure prevented the sessions from becoming complaint forums and kept them generative.

04 — Outcomes

A shared map that changed how teams work.

  • Complete end-to-end service blueprint delivered, owned jointly by all 6 teams
  • 55% reduction in cross-team dependency blockers within the following quarter
  • 4 previously invisible process gaps identified and assigned owners
  • Decision framework created to handle ambiguous handoffs — used beyond this product

The blueprint became a reference artifact that teams returned to during planning cycles, incident reviews, and onboarding. The real outcome wasn't a document — it was a shared language for talking about a system everyone had been inside but nobody had fully seen.
